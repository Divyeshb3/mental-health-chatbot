Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy for Depression

Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) is a structured program that combines mindfulness meditation with cognitive therapy techniques specifically designed to help people with depression.

How mindfulness helps depression:

1. Breaking the rumination cycle
Depression often involves repetitive negative thinking called rumination. Mindfulness teaches people to notice these thought patterns without getting caught up in them. By observing thoughts as passing mental events rather than facts, people with depression can interrupt the rumination cycle.

2. Recognising early warning signs
Mindfulness helps people become aware of the early signs of a depressive episode such as changes in mood, energy, sleep, and thinking patterns. This awareness allows them to take action before depression deepens.

3. Behavioural activation through mindfulness
When depressed, people tend to withdraw from activities. Mindful engagement with daily activities — eating, walking, washing — brings people back into contact with their lives and reduces withdrawal behaviours.

4. Reducing self-criticism
Depression is often accompanied by harsh self-judgment. Mindfulness cultivates self-compassion — treating oneself with the same kindness one would show a good friend. This reduces the self-critical thinking that maintains depression.

5. Staying present instead of ruminating about the past
Depression often involves dwelling on past failures and regrets. Mindfulness anchors attention to the present moment, reducing the time spent in unhelpful thought patterns about the past.

6. Body awareness
Depression has physical symptoms — fatigue, heaviness, tension. Mindfulness body scan practices help people notice and accept these physical sensations without judgment, reducing their distressing impact.

Research evidence
Studies show that MBCT reduces the risk of relapse in people who have had three or more episodes of depression by approximately 44 percent compared to usual care. It is recommended by the UK National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) as a treatment for recurrent depression.

Simple mindfulness practices for depression:
- 3 minute breathing space: pause three times a day to notice thoughts, feelings and body sensations
- Mindful walking: pay full attention to the physical sensations of walking
- Body scan: systematically move attention through the body noticing sensations without judgment
- Mindful routine activities: bring full attention to daily tasks like washing dishes or brushing teeth

Important note:
Mindfulness is a supportive practice and not a replacement for professional mental health treatment. If you are experiencing depression please speak with a qualified mental health professional.
